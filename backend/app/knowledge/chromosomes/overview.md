# Chromosomes — General Overview

Humans typically have 46 chromosomes, arranged in 23 pairs — one copy of
each pair inherited from each parent.

- **Autosomes**: chromosome pairs 1 through 22. These carry the vast
  majority of genes and are shared equally regardless of sex.
- **Sex chromosomes**: the 23rd pair. Typically XX in females and XY in
  males. Genes on the X chromosome behave differently in inheritance
  patterns than autosomal genes, because males only have one copy of
  the X chromosome.

## Why chromosome location matters for risk

- **Autosomal conditions** affect males and females equally, since both
  inherit two copies of every autosome.
- **X-linked conditions** often show a different pattern: a mother who
  is a carrier (heterozygous) has a 50% chance of passing the variant
  X chromosome to each child. Sons who inherit it are typically
  affected (since they have no second X to compensate), while daughters
  who inherit it are typically carriers themselves, often with milder
  or no symptoms, depending on the specific condition.

## Chromosomal abnormalities

Some conditions arise not from a variant within a single gene, but from
an abnormal number of whole chromosomes (aneuploidy) or structural
rearrangements (deletions, duplications, translocations). Down Syndrome
(Trisomy 21 — an extra copy of chromosome 21) is the most well-known
example of a full extra autosome.
