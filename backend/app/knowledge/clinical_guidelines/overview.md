# Clinical Guidelines — General Overview

Genetic counseling and carrier screening follow established professional
practice patterns, generally set by bodies such as the American College
of Medical Genetics and Genomics (ACMG) and the American College of
Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) in the US, with equivalent bodies
in other countries.

## General principles commonly reflected in guidelines

- **Informed, non-directive counseling**: genetic counselors present
  risk information and options neutrally, supporting the patient's own
  decision-making rather than recommending a particular choice.
- **Carrier screening before or during early pregnancy**: allows couples
  to understand recessive-condition risk to a pregnancy while there is
  still time to consider further testing or reproductive options.
- **Confirmatory testing**: a single screening result (including an
  AI-assisted risk estimate) is generally treated as a starting point
  for further discussion and, where appropriate, confirmatory clinical-
  grade genetic testing — not as a final diagnosis on its own.
- **Family history matters**: guidelines typically weigh known family
  history of a condition alongside genetic test results, since family
  history can reveal risk that a single genetic test might miss (e.g.
  variants not covered by a specific test panel).
- **Psychosocial support**: guidelines commonly emphasize offering
  emotional support and clear next-step resources alongside clinical
  results, given the sensitive nature of hereditary risk information.
