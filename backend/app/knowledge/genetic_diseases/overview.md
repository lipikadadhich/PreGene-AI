# Genetic Diseases — General Overview

Genetic diseases are conditions caused, in whole or in part, by changes
(variants) in a person's DNA. They fall into a few broad groups:

- **Single-gene (Mendelian) disorders**: caused by a variant in one
  specific gene. Examples include Cystic Fibrosis (CFTR gene, autosomal
  recessive), Sickle Cell Disease (HBB gene, autosomal recessive), and
  Huntington's Disease (HTT gene, autosomal dominant).
- **Chromosomal disorders**: caused by an abnormal number or structure
  of chromosomes, such as Down Syndrome (an extra copy of chromosome 21,
  also called Trisomy 21).
- **Multifactorial (complex) disorders**: influenced by variants in
  multiple genes together with environmental factors — most common
  conditions like type 2 diabetes and many cancers fall here.
- **Hereditary cancer syndromes**: caused by inherited variants in genes
  that normally help repair DNA or control cell growth, such as BRCA1
  and BRCA2, which significantly raise lifetime risk of breast, ovarian,
  and related cancers when a harmful (pathogenic) variant is present.

## Why inheritance pattern matters

Knowing whether a condition is autosomal recessive, autosomal dominant,
or X-linked changes the risk calculation for a couple planning a
pregnancy:

- **Autosomal recessive**: both parents must carry at least one copy of
  the variant for a child to be affected. Two carrier parents have a
  25% chance per pregnancy of an affected child, 50% chance of a carrier
  child, and 25% chance of an unaffected, non-carrier child.
- **Autosomal dominant**: a single copy of the variant, inherited from
  either parent, is enough to cause the condition. An affected parent
  has roughly a 50% chance of passing it to each child.
- **X-linked recessive**: the gene is on the X chromosome. Sons of
  carrier mothers have a higher chance of being affected than daughters,
  because sons only have one X chromosome.
