# Mutations — General Overview

A mutation (or genetic variant) is any change in a DNA sequence compared
to the typical reference sequence. Not all variants cause disease — many
are harmless (benign) or have no clear clinical effect (variants of
uncertain significance, or VUS). Clinically significant variants are
usually called "pathogenic" or "likely pathogenic."

## Common types of variants

- **Point mutation (substitution)**: a single DNA base is swapped for
  another (e.g. A→G). If it changes the resulting amino acid, it's
  called a missense variant; if it introduces a premature stop signal,
  it's a nonsense variant.
- **Insertion**: one or more extra bases are added into the sequence.
- **Deletion**: one or more bases are removed from the sequence.
- **Frameshift**: an insertion or deletion whose length isn't a multiple
  of three, which shifts how the rest of the sequence is read during
  protein production — often producing a non-functional protein.
- **Copy number variant (CNV)**: a larger segment of DNA, sometimes
  containing whole genes, is duplicated or deleted.

## Zygosity

Zygosity describes how many copies of a particular variant a person
carries at a given position:

- **Homozygous reference**: no copies of the variant (typical/typical).
- **Heterozygous**: one copy of the variant, one typical copy. In VCF
  genotype notation this is commonly written as 0/1 or 1/0.
- **Homozygous alternate**: two copies of the variant (1/1 in VCF
  notation) — the person carries the variant on both inherited copies
  of the gene.

Understanding zygosity is essential for recessive-condition risk
calculations, since two heterozygous (carrier) parents are the scenario
that creates meaningful risk for an affected child.
