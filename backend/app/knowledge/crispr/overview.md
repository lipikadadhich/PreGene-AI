# CRISPR — General Overview

CRISPR-Cas9 is a gene-editing technology adapted from a natural bacterial
defense system. It allows researchers to make precise, targeted changes
to DNA sequences inside living cells.

## How it works, at a high level

1. A short guide RNA (gRNA) is designed to match a specific target
   sequence in the genome.
2. The gRNA directs the Cas9 protein (an enzyme that cuts DNA) to that
   exact location.
3. Cas9 creates a double-strand break in the DNA at the targeted site.
4. The cell's own repair machinery then fixes the break — either by:
   - **Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)**: a faster, more error-prone
     repair that often disables the targeted gene — useful for "knocking
     out" a harmful gene.
   - **Homology-directed repair (HDR)**: a slower, more precise repair
     that can insert or correct a specific sequence if a repair template
     is provided — useful for correcting a disease-causing variant back
     to the typical sequence.

## Clinical maturity levels

Not all CRISPR-based approaches are equally far along. Broadly:

- **FDA-approved**: therapies that have completed clinical trials and
  received regulatory approval (e.g. a CRISPR-based therapy for certain
  forms of Sickle Cell Disease and Beta Thalassemia).
- **Clinical trial stage**: being tested in human patients, but not yet
  broadly approved.
- **Preclinical / experimental**: tested in cell or animal models, not
  yet in human trials.
- **Theoretical candidate**: a plausible target based on the disease's
  genetics, but no specific CRISPR strategy has been developed or
  tested yet.

## Important caveat

CRISPR gene editing for reproductive/embryo use (germline editing) is
distinct from somatic (body-cell) editing, is far more ethically and
regulatorily restricted, and is not currently an approved clinical
practice in essentially any country for hereditary disease prevention
in embryos.
