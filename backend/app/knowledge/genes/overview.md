# Genes — General Overview

A gene is a segment of DNA that contains the instructions for making a
specific protein or functional RNA molecule. Humans have roughly 20,000
protein-coding genes, packaged across 23 pairs of chromosomes.

## Alleles and genotype notation

Every person inherits two copies of most genes — one from each parent.
Each copy is called an **allele**. In simplified genotype notation
(often used in carrier-screening contexts):

- **AA** — both copies are the typical/reference allele (homozygous
  reference; not a carrier).
- **Aa** — one typical and one variant allele (heterozygous; a carrier
  for a recessive condition, but usually unaffected themselves).
- **aa** — both copies carry the variant allele (homozygous alternate;
  affected if the condition is recessive, or a more severe presentation
  in some dominant conditions).

## BRCA1 and BRCA2

BRCA1 and BRCA2 are two of the most well-studied genes in hereditary
cancer risk. Both normally function as tumor suppressors, helping repair
double-strand breaks in DNA. A harmful (pathogenic) variant in either
gene significantly increases lifetime risk of breast cancer, ovarian
cancer, and — for BRCA1 specifically — has also been associated with
increased risk of primary peritoneal cancer, a cancer that arises from
the lining of the abdominal cavity and is biologically related to
ovarian cancer.

## CFTR

CFTR is the gene responsible for Cystic Fibrosis. It codes for a protein
that regulates the movement of chloride ions across cell membranes.
Pathogenic variants disrupt this function, leading to thick, sticky
mucus buildup primarily affecting the lungs and digestive system.
Cystic Fibrosis is autosomal recessive.
